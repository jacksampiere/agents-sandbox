# ML Experiment Report — 2026-04-09

## 1. Experiment Trajectory

**exp_001 (Baseline):** A two-layer MLP [128, 64] with lr=0.01 achieved 72.4% val accuracy. Training loss was visibly jagged, suggesting the learning rate was slightly too large for late-stage convergence. The train/val gap of 0.15 was modest, indicating underfitting rather than overfitting as the primary limitation. The model was still improving at epoch 20.

**exp_002 (lr=0.001):** Dropping the learning rate 10× to 0.001 immediately paid off — val accuracy rose to 75.9% (+3.5pp), the loss curve smoothed considerably, and the train/val gap narrowed to 0.11. The model was still clearly improving at epoch 20, pointing to two bottlenecks: architecture capacity and training duration.

**exp_003 (lr=0.1):** Pushing the learning rate to 0.1 was a deliberate stress test that confirmed the failure mode. Val accuracy collapsed to 65.2%, val loss ballooned to 1.69, and the train/val gap widened to 0.26 — a clear instability signature. This experiment usefully closed off the high-lr search direction and bracketed the optimal lr between 0.001 and 0.01.

**exp_004 (Deeper network + regularization):** This was the first major inflection point. Widening and deepening the network to [256, 256, 128, 64], adding dropout=0.2 and weight_decay=1e-4, and extending training to 30 epochs drove val accuracy to 82.9% (+7pp over exp_002). Train loss dropped to 0.49 while val loss stabilized at 0.67 — a train/val gap of 0.18 that signaled the onset of mild overfitting. The architecture change was clearly the dominant driver; the capacity constraint hypothesis was confirmed.

**exp_005 (Data augmentation + more data):** The second major inflection point. Scaling training data from 10k to 25k samples, adding standard augmentation ops, and nudging dropout to 0.3 brought val accuracy to 87.3% (+4.4pp) and — critically — collapsed the train/val gap from 0.18 to 0.08. The overfitting signal from exp_004 was fully suppressed. Training remained stable through all 30 epochs with the model still improving at the end. This is the best run to date.

---

## 2. Key Patterns

- **Architecture capacity was the single largest lever.** The jump from exp_002 to exp_004 (+7pp) dwarfs every other single change. The two-layer baseline was clearly the binding constraint for the first three experiments.

- **Data volume and augmentation are highly effective regularizers here.** exp_005 shows that moving from 10k→25k samples with augmentation did more than dropout alone could (gap dropped from 0.18 → 0.08), while simultaneously improving accuracy by 4.4pp. This is the classic regime where more data improves both bias and variance simultaneously.

- **lr=0.001 is robustly the right order of magnitude; lr≥0.01 is harmful.** exp_003 rules out lr=0.1 decisively. exp_001 vs. exp_002 shows that even 0.01 introduces problematic noise. No learning rate scheduling has been tried — every run has used a flat lr, and every run has ended with "still improving," strongly suggesting a decay schedule would yield additional gains.

- **The model is consistently still improving at the final epoch.** All five experiments end with a positive val_acc_gain signal and a still-declining val loss. This is a persistent signal of undertrained models — the training budget (20–30 epochs, flat lr) has been the ceiling in every run.

- **Mild but growing overfitting as capacity scales.** exp_004's train/val gap (0.18) was wider than exp_002's (0.11) despite added dropout. exp_005 fixed this with data, not more dropout. If the model scales further, regularization strategy will need to keep pace — dropout alone was insufficient at p=0.2.

## 3. Recommendations

**Rank 1 — Add a learning rate scheduler (cosine decay or step decay) to exp_005's config**
- *What:* Keep all of exp_005's settings but add cosine annealing (e.g., decay from 0.001 to 1e-5 over 50–60 epochs) or a step decay at epoch 20 and 40.
- *Why:* Every single experiment has ended "still improving" with a flat lr. The consistently positive val_acc_gain_last3 signal (~0.009–0.024) across 30 epochs strongly implies the model is being prevented from reaching a better minimum by a fixed step size, not by capacity or data. This is the highest-confidence, lowest-cost intervention available.
- *Confirmation:* Val accuracy should exceed 89% and the final val_acc_gain should approach ~0.001–0.002 (convergence), rather than remaining above 0.005.

**Rank 2 — Extend training to 50–60 epochs alongside the scheduler**
- *What:* Run 50–60 epochs with cosine decay. Do not do this without the scheduler — a flat lr for 60 epochs will likely hurt.
- *Why:* Combined with Rank 1, longer training allows the scheduler to complete its full decay cycle. With the overfitting problem already solved by augmentation, there is no reason to stop early.
- *Confirmation:* Val loss should continue declining monotonically through epoch 40+ (currently it plateaus by ~25). If val loss starts rising before epoch 50, increase dropout from 0.3 → 0.4.

**Rank 3 — Switch architecture to a CNN (if the data is image-like)**
- *What:* Replace the MLP with a small CNN (e.g., 3–4 conv blocks with batch norm, followed by 1–2 FC layers). Keep all other settings from exp_005 (lr=0.001, augmentation, 25k samples).
- *Why:* The researcher explicitly raised this question in exp_005 notes. The task involves image augmentation ops (random_flip, random_crop, color_jitter), making it almost certainly an image classification problem. MLPs discard spatial structure, and a CNN would exploit it directly. The remaining gap to state-of-the-art is likely architectural.
- *Confirmation:* A CNN baseline under identical training conditions should reach ≥90% val accuracy. If it doesn't outperform the MLP by at least 3pp, the dataset may not have strong spatial structure and the MLP is the right inductive bias.

**Rank 4 — Batch normalization in the existing MLP**
- *What:* Add batch normalization after each linear layer (before activation) in the current [256, 256, 128, 64] architecture, keeping everything else from exp_005.
- *Why:* Batch norm often serves as an implicit regularizer, can allow slightly higher learning rates, and smooths the loss landscape. The current architecture has no normalization. This is a low-risk, high-upside addition that could close another 1–2pp before committing to a full architecture change (Rank 3).
- *Confirmation:* Val accuracy should improve to ~88.5–89.5% and training should be noticeably more stable (tighter epoch-to-epoch variance in val loss).

**Rank 5 — Ablate augmentation ops individually**
- *What:* Run three variants of exp_005 each removing one augmentation op: (a) no color_jitter, (b) no random_crop, (c) no random_horizontal_flip, keeping everything else fixed.
- *Why:* All three augmentation ops were added simultaneously in exp_005, so the individual contribution of each is unknown. If one op is harmful or neutral (e.g., random_horizontal_flip is inappropriate if the dataset has orientation-dependent labels), removing it could both improve accuracy and reduce training noise.
- *Confirmation:* If any single ablation reaches ≥87.5% val accuracy, that op is safe to drop, reducing training cost without hurting performance. If all three ablations fall below 86%, all ops are jointly necessary.
